**Leveraged lending documentation has undergone a structural transformation since 2020**, driven by the explosion of liability management transactions, landmark court rulings (Serta, Mitel, Incora), and an ongoing tug-of-war between borrower flexibility and lender protections. This knowledge base synthesizes the current state of credit agreement practice across ten critical domains — from EBITDA add-back mechanics to the post-Serta litigation landscape — drawing on LSTA and LMA publications, leading law firm analyses, and industry data from Octus/Covenant Review, PitchBook LCD, and court decisions through early 2026.

## 1. Documentation trends reveal a market in tension [US]

U.S. total leveraged finance volume **doubled to $653.4 billion in 2024** from $329.6 billion in 2023, with institutional volumes surging 113% to $500.2 billion [VERIFY: time-sensitive — leveraged finance volume data updated annually]. Refinancing dominated — 72% of loans in Q4 2024 were refinancing transactions, and over 60% of outstanding leveraged loans were refinanced, repriced, or repaid during the year. New money accounted for only 15% of roughly $1.3 trillion in total 2024 leveraged lending. By Q3 2025, U.S. leveraged loan issuance hit $544.9 billion in a single quarter — the highest on Debtwire record — with the average institutional loan margin falling to a record low of **3.13%** [VERIFY: time-sensitive — margin data changes with market conditions] (Source: PitchBook LCD; White & Case Debt Explorer, Q3 2025; ICLG Lending & Secured Finance 2025–2026).

**Covenant-lite remains dominant.** Approximately 90% of new BSL loans in 2024 remained covenant-lite, continuing the long-term borrower-friendly trend [VERIFY: time-sensitive — cov-lite share tracked annually by PitchBook LCD]. Competition from private credit has pushed some lenders to remove covenants for borrowers with EBITDA as low as $30 million, below the traditional $50 million threshold (Source: Paul Weiss/Practical Law, 2025). Even in private credit, fewer than 10% of loans above $500 million include maintenance covenants (Source: Resonanz Capital, 2025). However, private credit deals retain notably stronger covenant packages overall. The LSTA's Q1 2025 Covenant Trends report observed that LME blocker language, tighter EBITDA definitions, and stronger protective provisions appeared more frequently in private credit than in BSL.

**Borrower-friendly provisions that continued loosening through 2024–2025 include:** more expansive EBITDA adjustment definitions, larger grower baskets, expanded reclassification provisions allowing basket-stacking, more incurrence-based carve-outs to debt caps, greater collateral leakage to non-loan parties, and a surge in dividend recapitalizations (15.6% of U.S. loan proceeds in 2024 versus 5.6% globally in 2023) [VERIFY: time-sensitive — dividend recap share data from LSTA Covenant Trends] (Source: LSTA Covenant Trends 4Q24; ICLG 2025).

**Areas of successful lender pushback** include EBITDA adjustment caps (present in 86% of deals, typically at 25% of EBITDA), look-forward periods for projected synergies, and — most significantly — LME blocker provisions. Serta/uptier blockers appeared in **70.9% of loans** as of Q2 2024 per LSTA data [VERIFY: time-sensitive — blocker adoption rates from LSTA Covenant Trends], and J.Crew blockers rose to 39% in high-yield bond deals, up from 30% in 2023 (Source: Octus; LSTA Covenant Trends 2Q24). By early 2025, lender flex increased marginally as supply waned, with covenant protections shifting slightly in lenders' favor.

### LSTA and LMA model form updates (2024–2025)

The LSTA published several significant updates [US]. Its **Emerging Business Credit Agreement (EBCA)**, released July 8, 2024, is the LSTA's first-ever bilateral credit agreement form, designed for companies between early-stage ventures and middle-market borrowers. Updated **Model Credit Agreement Provisions (MCAPs)** — most recently finalized on May 1, 2023 (reposted July 8, 2024, with corrections) — had subsequent draft revisions published in February, April, and June 2025, with the latter revisions focused on Article 17 (confidentiality). Latin American Cross-Border MCAPs were updated December 6, 2024 for Chile and other jurisdictions. On September 24, 2024, the LSTA issued representative best-practice liability management transaction protections covering Serta, J.Crew, Pluralsight, Envision, At Home, and Chewy-type transactions.

On the LMA side [UK/EU], the **Africa Term SOFR Facility Agreement** was published as an exposure draft on July 24, 2024 — the first multi-jurisdictional African facility agreement covering seven countries under English law. LMA Sustainability-Linked Loan bolt-on provisions were published for the leveraged facilities agreement. Core LMA leveraged facilities agreement updates are available only to members and could not be fully confirmed from public sources [VERIFY: event-driven — LMA member-only publications may have updated since last review].

**Regulatory developments** [INTERNATIONAL] include IOSCO's June 2024 publication of "Leveraged Loans and CLOs: Good Practices for Consideration," which specifically called out EBITDA adjustment complexity and covenant erosion. The ECB conducted a 2024 review identifying risks in covenant-lite structures and signaled expectations for more conservative practices in 2025 (Source: IOSCO FR03/2024; Chambers Debt Finance 2025).

## 2. EBITDA add-backs inflate leverage and obscure credit risk

The gap between reported and credit-agreement-adjusted EBITDA remains one of the most consequential issues in leveraged lending. **S&P Global's sixth annual EBITDA Addback Study (March 2024)** found that issuers' projected adjusted EBITDA at deal inception exceeded actual realized EBITDA by **approximately 30% on average** over the following two years [VERIFY: time-sensitive — S&P publishes annual addback study updates]. For the 2022 vintage, add-backs represented over 29% of management-projected EBITDA and almost **55% of LTM reported EBITDA**. The 2020 vintage showed median leverage 2.1 turns higher than forecast for 2021 and 3 turns higher for 2022. Higher add-backs at inception correlate with more severe projection misses.

Hamilton Lane's 2024 analysis found that 2021 vintage deals levered above 6.0× on an adjusted EBITDA basis could be **above 8.0× levered off true cash flow**, presenting significant credit risk in a higher-for-longer rate environment (Source: Hamilton Lane Private Credit Viewpoints 2024).

### Standard add-back categories and cap conventions

Standard add-backs in leveraged loan credit agreements include: non-cash charges and losses (stock-based compensation, impairment, unrealized losses, amortization); extraordinary, unusual, or non-recurring items; restructuring and business optimization expenses (typically subject to a percentage cap); transaction fees and expenses; pro forma cost savings and synergies (the most controversial category); business interruption insurance proceeds; and amounts supported by Quality of Earnings reports (Source: Haynes Boone/Practical Law, 2024; Proskauer).

**Cap conventions have tightened.** EBITDA adjustment caps appeared in 86% of 2024 deals, with a typical cap of **25% of EBITDA** in large-cap deals — described as "fast becoming the norm." Mid-market caps have migrated from the once-standard 10–15% range to 20%, with 25% appearing in select transactions. Uncapped adjustments persist in some of the largest deals with the strongest sponsors but face increasing investor resistance. A critical negotiation point is whether the cap percentage is calculated before or after giving effect to the add-backs — the post-adjustment calculation is more borrower-favorable (Source: Octus/ICLG 2025; Osborne Clarke; Haynes Boone 2024).

**Synergy add-back time horizons** are standard at **12 to 24 months** from the relevant event. Key negotiation points include whether actions must have been taken, initiated, or merely "expected to be taken" — the last being the most borrower-favorable and controversial. Critically, most formulations impose no requirement that synergies actually be realized within any timeframe, only that they are projected within the look-forward period. Tiered certification requirements ratchet up with each percentage increase in EBITDA, with the highest tier potentially requiring independent third-party certification — though these are often "couched behind numerous assumptions and reservations" (Source: Proskauer; Osborne Clarke).

Academic research from the University of Houston's Bauer College found that loans with above-median add-backs show higher leverage, lower asset tangibility, worse credit quality, and increased likelihood of delinquency, default, and ratings downgrade — and that this higher risk is **not fully priced into loan spreads** (Source: Bauer College, UH).

## 3. Erroneous payment provisions are now market standard after Citibank/Revlon

On August 11, 2020, Citibank — acting as administrative agent for a $1.8 billion Revlon syndicated loan — accidentally wired approximately **$894 million** of its own funds to 315 lenders when it intended to send only a $7.8 million interest payment. The error arose from a software misconfiguration in Citibank's Oracle/Flexcube system, despite a three-person "six-eye" approval process. About 200 lenders returned roughly $385 million, but ten loan managers refused to return over $500 million, noting that Revlon was deeply insolvent and its loan participations were trading at **20–30 cents on the dollar**.

### The litigation produced landmark precedent [US]

**District Court (February 16, 2021):** Judge Jesse Furman, calling the error "one of the biggest blunders in banking history," ruled for the defendant lenders under the **"discharge-for-value" defense** from *Banque Worms v. BankAmerica Int'l* (1991). The court found lenders were owed exactly the amounts received, did not induce the transfers, and were not on notice of the mistake.

**Second Circuit (September 8, 2022):** A unanimous three-judge panel **vacated** the district court on two independent grounds. First, the court adopted an **"inquiry notice" standard**, identifying four red flags that placed lenders on constructive notice: no prior notice of prepayment (contractually required), Revlon's deep insolvency, steep-discount trading of the loans, and Revlon's exchange offer launched just four days earlier. Second, the court held lenders had **no "present entitlement"** — the loan was not due for three more years and had not been accelerated. Judge Park's concurrence called the case "straightforward" and "grossly overcomplicated." Rehearing was denied, and LSTA panelists agreed the case was unlikely to reach the Supreme Court given it involved state rather than federal law (Source: Cadwalader, September 2022; Torys LLP; Choate Hall & Stewart).

**Settlement (December 2022):** All ten creditors signed an agreement to return the money. Roughly three-quarters of the mistaken payments were returned. The case was dismissed on December 19, 2022. Separately, the Federal Reserve and OCC fined Citibank **$400 million** and ordered risk management improvements (Source: Bloomberg; Banking Dive, December 2022).

### The LSTA erroneous payment provision [US]

The LSTA published initial model language on March 19, 2021, and issued a revised draft on June 16, 2021, drafted by LSTA General Counsel Bridget Marsh. The provision became **effectively market standard almost immediately**. Cleary Gottlieb noted in December 2022 that "the vast majority of US market participants have incorporated some version" into their forms. Key terms include: the administrative agent has **sole discretion** to determine whether a payment was erroneous (conclusive absent manifest error); lenders must return funds within **two business days** plus interest; if a payment differs from a payment notice or arrives without a prepayment notice, an error is **presumed** and the lender must notify the agent within one business day; lenders **expressly waive** the discharge-for-value defense; the agent has contractual **subrogation** rights; and the agent may **set off** unreturned amounts against future payments. An optional clawback cutoff date of 5 business days was included but most deals either omitted it or set periods of 3 to 30 days (Source: ABA/Bridget Marsh; Akin Gump; Seward & Kissel).

### The LMA erroneous payment provision [UK/EU]

The **LMA published its optional erroneous payment clause** on June 30, 2021. Key differences from the LSTA version reflect the more favorable English law restitution framework: no presumption-of-error language (since English law's "good consideration" defense requires actual knowledge, not constructive notice), no deemed assignment or subrogation mechanism, and generally simpler structure. Many English law facility agreements adopted the LMA provision verbatim, usually dispensing with the optional clawback time limit (Source: Cleary Gottlieb JIBFL, December 2022; Deacons, August 2021).

**No specific legislation** addressing the discharge-for-value defense in wire transfers has been enacted [US]. The primary response has been contractual — through the LSTA and LMA model provisions. Current New York UCC amendments (S1840A/A3307A) address digital assets and controllable electronic records, not erroneous payments [VERIFY: event-driven — monitor for any subsequent legislative developments addressing erroneous payments].

## 4. Liability management transactions reshaped the credit landscape [US]

The rise of "creditor-on-creditor violence" — where majority lenders cooperate with borrowers to prime or subordinate minority lenders — has produced the most significant wave of credit agreement litigation in a generation. Seven landmark transactions and their judicial outcomes define the current legal landscape. All seven cases discussed below arise under US law; while European documentation increasingly incorporates LME protections (see § 8 on LSTA/LMA convergence), the UK/EU has not yet produced comparable LME litigation [UK/EU].

### Serta Simmons: the Fifth Circuit closes the open market loophole [US]

In June 2020, Serta executed an uptier exchange: $200 million in new first-out superpriority financing plus $1.2 billion in existing loans exchanged for $875 million in second-out superpriority debt, with the exchange characterized as an "open market purchase." The excluded lenders sued. A New York state court declined a preliminary injunction; Judge Failla in the SDNY denied dismissal in March 2022, finding the exchange was not "conventionally understood" as an open market transaction.

After Serta's January 2023 bankruptcy filing, Judge David Jones ruled the uptier was a valid open market purchase. But the **Fifth Circuit unanimously reversed on December 31, 2024**, holding the term requires purchase "on the secondary market for syndicated loans" — not via private negotiation. The court relied on dictionary definitions, LSTA publications, and the surplusage doctrine (the Dutch Auction alternative would be rendered superfluous). The court also **excised the settlement indemnity** from the confirmed plan under §502(e)(1)(B). Rehearing was denied February 18, 2025. Trial on excluded lenders' breach of contract counterclaims was tentatively set for February 2026 [VERIFY: event-driven — trial date may have been rescheduled] (Source: *In re Serta Simmons Bedding, LLC*, 125 F.4th 555 (5th Cir. 2024); Mayer Brown, May 2025).

### Other key transactions and their outcomes [US]

**J.Crew (2016–2017):** Exploited a "trap door" to transfer 72% of collected trademarks from a loan party through a restricted subsidiary to an unrestricted subsidiary, which used the IP as collateral for new debt. The key loophole was a "proceeds" basket lacking a definition of "proceeds." Settlement required approximately 88% of secured lenders to ratify the transaction in exchange for selling $150 million of loans at par (trading at ~70 cents). J.Crew filed Chapter 11 in May 2020. The transaction spawned ubiquitous **"J.Crew blocker" provisions**.

**Boardriders/Quiksilver (August 2020):** Participating lenders holding $321 million (71%) consented to $110 million in new superpriority debt, with existing loans exchanged at par (trading at 50–60 cents). Justice Masley denied dismissal in October 2022, finding the sacred rights provision could be read to prohibit lien subordination and that "open market purchase" was ambiguous. Settled in August 2023 without precedent-setting decision.

**TriMark (September 2020):** Structurally similar to Boardriders. Justice Cohen denied dismissal in August 2021, finding potential sacred rights violations. Settled in January 2022 with all first-lien debt exchanged dollar-for-dollar for superpriority Tranche B Loans — preventing another precedent-setting decision.

**Incora/Wesco Aircraft (March 2022):** A particularly aggressive multi-step transaction. PIMCO and Silver Point amended indentures by simple majority to issue additional 2026 Notes, achieving supermajority voting position, then stripped liens from non-participating noteholders. Judge Isgur's **"falling dominos" framework** (July 2024) found the uptier violated the 2026 Notes indenture — the first amendment "had the effect of releasing all or substantially all" of the collateral. However, **Chief District Judge Crane reversed on December 8, 2025**, holding transactions were "proper, appropriate, lawful, and consistent" with indenture terms — each step complied at the time executed. **Minority noteholders filed a notice of appeal to the Fifth Circuit on January 7, 2026.** Appellate briefing is expected to extend through mid-to-late 2026, with oral argument potentially in late 2026 or 2027. The key appellate question is whether the Fifth Circuit's December 2024 *Serta* ruling compels the same result, or whether the different indenture language (lacking the same "sacred right" protections as a credit agreement) is dispositive. [VERIFY: event-driven — monitor Fifth Circuit docket for briefing schedule and any expedited consideration] (Source: S.D. Tex., December 2025; Law360, January 2026; Cleary Gottlieb analysis).

**Mitel Networks (October 2022):** The credit agreement allowed borrower purchases "at any time" without an "open market" qualifier. The NY Appellate Division, First Department **dismissed all claims on December 31, 2024** (the same day as the Serta Fifth Circuit ruling), holding the uptier was a permissible "purchase" and that non-participating lenders were only "indirectly" affected. Mitel filed pre-negotiated Chapter 11 in March 2025 and emerged in April 2025.

**Robertshaw (May 2023):** Featured a rare creditor-on-creditor dispute — after an initial uptier, the ad hoc group conducted a second LME stripping Invesco of Required Lender status. Judge Lopez found Robertshaw **breached** the credit agreement by incurring unauthorized debt but limited the remedy to monetary damages, declining to unwind the transaction. Robertshaw emerged from Chapter 11 in October 2024.

### Post-Serta LME activity has not slowed

Despite the Fifth Circuit's ruling, LME transactions continued through 2025. New workaround structures include: amending the "open market purchase" provision by majority vote to add "privately negotiated" purchase language (adopting the Mitel approach); "extend-and-exchange" two-step maneuvers exploiting the limitation of pro rata sharing to same-class loans; and proliferating cooperation agreements as a creditor defensive tool. Borrowers responded with attempts at "anti-cooperation" provisions — Octus documented at least 13 attempts in 2025, with only 3 successful (Warner Bros. Discovery, Getty Images, Optimum Communications) [VERIFY: time-sensitive — anti-cooperation provision adoption tracked by Octus] (Source: Dechert, June 2025; Octus LME Year in Review, 2025).

### Anti-Serta blockers and the evolving taxonomy

The LME blocker taxonomy has expanded to seven distinct categories identified by White & Case (October 2025): **J.Crew/Pluralsight** (dropdown), **Envision** (aggregate cap on basket stacking), **At Home/Sabre** (double-dip), **Chewy/PetSmart** (guarantor release), **Wesco/Incora** (vote rigging), **Serta** (uptier requiring affected lender consent), and **omni-blocker** (catch-all prohibition on non-pro rata LMEs). In EMEA loans, approximately 90% include anti-Serta provisions [UK/EU]; in EMEA high-yield bonds, only about 13% [VERIFY: time-sensitive — EMEA adoption rates from Octus/Reorg H1 2025 data]. CreditSights has flagged that many existing blockers contain exceptions rendering protections "essentially meaningless" — including blanket exceptions for priming revolvers and structuring fee carve-outs.

**LME Blocker Taxonomy:**

| Blocker Type | Protects Against | Key Mechanism | Prevalence (2024) |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Serta | Uptier exchange / superpriority debt | Requires affected lender consent for non-pro-rata debt exchange | ~71% of loans (LSTA Q2 2024) |
| J.Crew/Pluralsight | IP/asset dropdown to unrestricted subsidiary | Restricts transfer of material assets outside restricted group | ~39% of HY bonds |
| Envision | Aggregate basket stacking to create capacity | Caps cumulative basket utilization | Growing adoption |
| At Home/Sabre | Double-dip claims at multiple structural levels | Restricts guarantor structures enabling duplicate claims | Growing adoption |
| Chewy/PetSmart | Guarantor release to eliminate credit support | Restricts release of material guarantors | Growing adoption |
| Wesco/Incora | Vote manipulation to achieve supermajority | Restricts amendments to voting thresholds | Growing adoption |
| Omni-blocker | All non-pro-rata LME transactions | Catch-all prohibition | Newest category |

### Agent Operational Guide to Liability Management Transactions

The legal analysis in the preceding sections describes what LME transactions are and how courts have ruled on them. This section addresses the operational reality: what does the administrative agent DO when an LME is proposed?

**Step 1: Identify the transaction type and applicable provisions**
When the agent receives a consent solicitation or amendment request that involves non-pro-rata treatment of lenders, the agent must first classify the transaction:
- Is this an uptier exchange (Serta-type)? Look for: new superpriority debt, existing loans exchanged at non-pro-rata terms, 'open market purchase' language invoked
- Is this a dropdown (J.Crew/Pluralsight-type)? Look for: asset transfers to unrestricted subsidiaries, new debt incurred at the subsidiary level
- Is this a double-dip (At Home/Sabre-type)? Look for: guarantor structures that allow claims at multiple levels
- Is this a vote manipulation (Wesco/Incora-type)? Look for: amendments to achieve supermajority voting position before a subsequent amendment

**Step 2: Determine the correct consent threshold**
Map each element of the transaction to the credit agreement's amendment provisions:
- Which provisions are being amended? (Identify the specific sections)
- Are any sacred rights implicated? (Maturity extension, rate reduction, pro rata sharing, collateral release, voting threshold changes)
- What is the Required Lender threshold? (Typically >50% of commitments/outstandings)
- Are 'all affected lender' or 'each lender directly and adversely affected' provisions triggered?
- Does the credit agreement require supermajority (66⅔% or higher) for any of the proposed changes?

**Step 3: Check for anti-LME blocker provisions**
Review the credit agreement for the seven blocker categories identified by White & Case (October 2025):
- Serta blocker (uptier requiring affected lender consent)
- J.Crew/Pluralsight blocker (dropdown restrictions)
- Envision blocker (aggregate cap on basket stacking)
- At Home/Sabre blocker (double-dip prevention)
- Chewy/PetSmart blocker (guarantor release restrictions)
- Wesco/Incora blocker (vote rigging prevention)
- Omni-blocker (catch-all prohibition on non-pro-rata LMEs)

Note: CreditSights has flagged that many existing blockers contain exceptions rendering protections 'essentially meaningless' — including blanket exceptions for priming revolvers and structuring fee carve-outs. The agent should read blocker provisions carefully, including their exceptions.

**Step 4: Manage agent neutrality**
The agent's obligation is to the ENTIRE syndicate, not to any subset. When a cooperating group approaches the agent:
- Do NOT provide confidential information about non-participating lenders' positions
- Do NOT facilitate communications between the cooperating group and the borrower that exclude other lenders (unless the credit agreement expressly permits this)
- DO process the consent solicitation in accordance with the credit agreement's amendment provisions
- DO distribute all notices and information to ALL lenders simultaneously (unless the credit agreement creates information classifications)
- DO consult external legal counsel — LME transactions create significant litigation risk for the agent

**Step 5: Document everything**
Every communication, every notice, every consent, and every instruction related to an LME transaction should be documented and retained. The agent may be called as a witness or named as a party in subsequent litigation. Maintain:
- Complete records of all notices sent and received
- Voting records and consent tabulations
- Communications with counsel
- Timeline of all actions taken
- Any instances where the agent flagged concerns or sought indemnification

**Agent indemnification:**
Before processing any LME transaction, the agent should verify that its indemnification provisions in the credit agreement are adequate. Consider requesting enhanced indemnification from the cooperating group, particularly if the transaction is likely to result in litigation. The agent's exposure in LME situations is among the highest in syndicated lending.

## 5. Disqualified lender lists have become a tactical weapon [US]

DQ lists have expanded far beyond their original purpose of preventing competitor access to confidential information. Lists now frequently include **distressed debt investors, special situations funds, and "loan-to-own" funds** — any investor perceived as potentially adversarial in a restructuring. Some credit agreements include catch-all language such as "any person whose primary activity is the trading or acquisition of distressed debt." Lists may now contain hundreds of names, and post-closing amendment rights have expanded: many agreements allow borrowers to add non-competitor entities after closing with prospective effect. A striking new development: **"disqualified counsel" provisions** give borrowers the right to name one law firm that lenders cannot retain (Source: Alston & Bird, July 2023; AIMA, 2024; Octus, 2025).

The LSTA MCAPs recommend DQ lists be limited to three categories [US]: persons designated at or before the commitment letter date; competitors designated post-closing (limited to competitors only); and affiliates of the above. Key LSTA principles include: the admin agent posts the DQ list on the public side of the lender platform, has no liability for monitoring, updates take effect after 3 business days with no retroactive application, and transfers to DQ lenders do not automatically void but trigger borrower remedies (restrict information access, strip voting rights, buy back at par or acquisition price). A **bona fide debt fund** carve-out exempts affiliates of competitors operating as passive financial investors (Source: LSTA MCAPs; Orrick, June 2022; Crowell & Moring, 2025). LMA documentation does not include a directly comparable standardized DQ list framework, though European deals increasingly adopt US-style DQ provisions in large-cap leveraged transactions [UK/EU].

Key litigation examples include **Serta** (Apollo affiliate allegedly circumvented DQ list; settled with 50% of loans cleared and 50% voided), **Byju's v. Glas Trust** (borrower invoked catch-all DQ language to block acceleration), and **PSSI/Blackstone** (sponsor designated Strategic Value Partners as DQ post-trade to block settlement). DQ lists have become central to the LME toolkit — sponsors use them to prevent distressed funds from acquiring blocking positions, to control voting blocs ahead of proposed amendments, and to force DQ entities to sell at the lesser of outstanding principal and acquisition price (Source: ICLG 2024–2025; AIMA; Creditor Rights Coalition).

## 6. Amendment mechanics and sacred rights are being rewritten in real time

The standard **"Required Lenders" threshold** [US] is more than 50% of aggregate commitments or outstandings (effectively 50.01%), with defaulting lenders and borrower-held loans excluded from the denominator. In the LMA European market [UK/EU], the standard "Majority Lenders" threshold is **66⅔%** for leveraged deals — a significantly higher bar. Some US club deals require the lead lender to be part of any Required Lenders vote (Source: LSTA forms; ICMA, November 2022; Proskauer).

**Traditional sacred rights** requiring unanimous or all-affected-lender consent cover: extending maturity or delaying payments; reducing interest or fees; increasing commitments; releasing all or substantially all collateral or guarantors; changing voting thresholds or the amendments section itself; and modifying pro rata sharing of payments and collateral proceeds. The critical distinction between "each lender directly and adversely affected" and "all lenders" has been exploited in LME contexts — effects on non-participating lenders were characterized as merely "indirect" in the Mitel ruling, avoiding the consent trigger (Source: Vorys, Fall 2022; Chapman & Cutler). LMA documentation includes a "snooze/lose" provision [UK/EU] (not typical in US) and super-majority tiers at 80–90% for collateral and guarantor releases.

**Post-LME sacred rights expansions** include: explicit coverage of lien subordination (not just release), broader pro rata sharing provisions covering open market purchases and privately negotiated transactions, supermajority thresholds (66⅔% or 85%) replacing simple majority for amending blocker provisions, addition of "directly or indirectly" language, and "have the effect of" language for collateral release provisions (Source: O'Melveny; White & Case, October 2025).

**Sacred Rights Summary:**

| Sacred Right | Consent Required | Typical US Threshold | Typical LMA Threshold |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Extend maturity / delay payments | Each affected lender | Each directly affected | Each affected + snooze/lose |
| Reduce interest rate or fees | Each affected lender | Each directly affected | Each affected |
| Increase commitments | Each affected lender | Each directly affected | Each affected |
| Release all/substantially all collateral | All lenders | All lenders | 80-90% super-majority |
| Release all/substantially all guarantors | All lenders | All lenders | 80-90% super-majority |
| Change voting thresholds | All lenders | All lenders | All lenders |
| Modify pro rata sharing | All lenders | All lenders | All lenders |
| Amend the amendments section | All lenders | All lenders | All lenders |
| Subordinate liens | Post-LME: each affected | Expanding adoption | Expanding adoption |

**Yank-a-bank provisions** allow borrowers to replace lenders that default, become insolvent, request increased costs, or refuse to approve amendments approved by Required Lenders on sacred rights matters. The replaced lender assigns at par to a replacement Eligible Assignee. Seldom deployed in practice, these provisions function primarily as a deterrent to holdout behavior (Source: Seward & Kissel, June 2021; Cadwalader, July 2020).

The **open market purchase loophole** — historically undefined in credit agreements — was substantially narrowed by the Fifth Circuit's Serta ruling [US] but remains viable where credit agreements use plain "purchase" language without an "open market" qualifier (per Mitel). Post-Serta, practitioners recommend: deleting the open market exception entirely, permitting it only if offered to all lenders on equal terms, requiring cash-only purchases, or explicitly defining the term. The ABA has called for standard definitions including arm's-length, pro rata access, cash consideration, and cancellation requirements (Source: ABA Business Law Today, December 2023; Cleary Gottlieb, January 2025).

## 7. Negative covenants have evolved into complex multi-layered baskets

### Builder baskets and restricted payment capacity

**Builder baskets (CNI-based)** appeared in over 91% of 2024 European SFAs [UK/EU], with 83% including an immediately available "starter amount." Three formulations exist: traditional (50% CNI reduced by 100% of losses), zero-floor (basket cannot go negative), and "no negative CNI" (continuous growth ignoring losses — the most borrower-favorable, now facing pushback). The most common financial condition test for accessing the basket is a **2× Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio**, required in approximately 85% of 2024 European SFAs [VERIFY: time-sensitive — European SFA covenant data from Xtract Research]. The separate **Available Amount basket** concept (migrating from US high-yield bonds) [US] appeared in 11% of 2024 European deals, up from 2% in 2023 (Source: ICLG 2025–2026 citing Xtract Research and Octus).

Restricted payment capacity and leakage remain difficult to quantify. Value leakage operates through multiple channels: dividend-to-debt toggles ("pick-your-poison" provisions, present in 26% of European leveraged loans in 2024), leverage-based step-downs for asset sale proceeds (74% of deals, up from 58%), dedicated permitted payment baskets for asset dispositions, and capacity cross-utilization allowing reallocation across baskets. The decline of no-default blockers in some covenants allows issuers to access dividend capacity even while in default (Source: ELFA; ICLG 2025–2026; Xtract).

### J.Crew blockers and unrestricted subsidiary flexibility

J.Crew blocker provisions are now "so ubiquitous that little effort is spent negotiating whether or not to incorporate them" in some form (Source: ABA, March 2024). They rose to **39% in HY bond deals** in 2024 from 30% in 2023 [VERIFY: time-sensitive — J.Crew blocker adoption data from Octus]. Sponsors have largely stopped resisting in private credit deals, and newer Pluralsight and Envision protections are gaining acceptance (Source: Octus; PitchBook/Proskauer, February 2026). Key limitations persist: they typically cover only IP (not physical assets or contracts), "material IP" is often poorly defined, and implementation varies widely.

Unrestricted subsidiary designation remains a significant flexibility tool. Approximately half of credit agreements still require all significant subsidiaries to be restricted, but unrestricted subsidiary capacity is now common in large-cap European and US leveraged documentation. Post-J.Crew exploits by Neiman Marcus, Cirque du Soleil, Travelport, PetSmart, and Party City demonstrated the leakage risk (Source: Orrick; Yale Law Journal; Proskauer).

### Grower baskets are expanding

Grower baskets — formulated as the greater of a fixed dollar amount and a percentage of EBITDA or total assets — appeared in **96% of 2024 deals** (up from 90% in 2023) [VERIFY: time-sensitive — grower basket prevalence from CovenantReview]. The average free-and-clear basket increased from 73% to **78% of pro forma adjusted EBITDA** year-over-year. "High-water marking" (where the fixed amount ratchets up to match peak EBITDA but never decreases) appeared in 38% of 2024 European SFAs and 24% of European HY bonds (up from 9% in 2023) (Source: Octus/CovenantReview; Xtract; ICLG 2025–2026).

## 8. LSTA and LMA documentation differ in architecture, not just geography [US/UK/EU]

The fundamental structural differences between US (New York law, LSTA) and European (English law, LMA) credit agreements reflect distinct legal traditions, though convergence is accelerating.

**Agency structure:** US deals [US] use an **administrative agent** coordinating notices, payments, and compliance, with a separate **collateral agent** holding security interests directly for lenders' benefit — no trust required under New York law. European deals [UK/EU] use a **security trustee** holding security on trust for a fluctuating body of beneficiaries, supplemented by a **parallel debt** structure where security is granted to the security agent in its individual capacity to secure a mirror obligation. This side-steps problems arising from novation-based transfers but varies in efficacy across European jurisdictions (Source: Milbank, IFLR December/January 2010; Lucosky Brookman, 2023).

**Transfer mechanics:** US credit agreements [US] use **assignment and assumption**, which under New York law does not terminate and recreate obligations — security is preserved. European documentation [UK/EU] offers both **novation** (transfer certificates terminating and recreating obligations, with potential security priority implications) and **assignment** (preserving security). The LMA settlement waterfall defaults to participation if the preferred method fails. A key difference: LMA participations create a debtor-creditor relationship (participant is an unsecured creditor of the grantor), while LSTA participations are intended as true sales of beneficial interest (Source: Milbank; Hunton Andrews Kurth; Crowell & Moring).

**Voting thresholds diverge significantly.** The LSTA standard [US] is simple majority (>50%); the LMA standard [UK/EU] is **66⅔%** for most amendments. LMA documentation includes a "snooze/lose" provision (not typical in US) and super-majority tiers at 80–90% for collateral and guarantor releases. Sacred rights are conceptually similar but scope varies (Source: Clifford Chance, 2015; Cravath/Slaughter and May, LMA News H1 2018).

**"Certain funds"** [UK/EU] is a defining feature of European acquisition finance. Under the UK City Code on Takeovers and Mergers, purchasers of listed UK companies must have committed financing at announcement with severely limited lender withdrawal rights. This has extended to private buyouts. The US equivalent [US] relies on **commitment papers** with "SunGard" limited-conditionality provisions (named after the 2005 SunGard Data Systems deal) (Source: Skadden ICLG 2022).

**Convergence is significant.** European sponsors routinely access the US institutional market via "Yankee loans." Large-cap European leveraged deals now commonly adopt NY law-governed, HY bond-style covenant packages as schedules to an English law LMA-form loan agreement. Convergence areas include J.Crew blockers, DQ lists, equity cure mechanics, cov-lite structures, and incremental facility provisions. The LSTA and LMA cooperate on regulatory developments and jointly published guidance on green and sustainability-linked loan products. However, differing insolvency regimes (US Chapter 11 versus European out-of-court enforcement) limit wholesale import of US concepts (Source: Skadden ICLG 2022; Clifford Chance, 2015; White & Case Debt Explorer).

## 9. Events of default provisions balance sensitivity with operational reality

### Standard categories and cure periods [US]

Standard EOD categories in leveraged loan credit agreements include: non-payment; inaccuracy of representations and warranties; breach of covenants (affirmative and negative); cross-default or cross-acceleration; insolvency (voluntary and involuntary); judgment defaults; ERISA events; environmental events; change of control; invalidity of guarantees or liens; and, less commonly, material adverse change.

**Payment default cure periods** follow a firm convention: **3–5 business days** for interest and fee payments; **no grace period** for principal (immediate EOD). Non-monetary covenant defaults typically receive **10–30 days**, often starting from lender notice. Involuntary bankruptcy is not an EOD unless the action produces an order not dismissed within **60–90 days** (Source: Olshan Law; Mayer Brown/Lexology; A.Y. Strauss).

### Equity cure rights bridge BSL and private credit

Equity cure rights are **near-universal in private credit** sponsor-backed deals with financial maintenance covenants but are **largely irrelevant in BSL** due to covenant-lite dominance. Key mechanics include: maximum cures typically capped at **4–5 times** over the facility life; frequency limited to no cures in consecutive quarters (European mid-market) [UK/EU] or no more than 2 in any 12-month period (US standard) [US]; equity contributions treated as dollar-for-dollar increases to EBITDA for the applicable measurement period; and scope limitations requiring cure contributions be disregarded for other purposes (pricing ratchets, grower baskets, excess cash flow calculations). Anti-round-tripping provisions ensure builder baskets do not "build" with cure equity. A notable trend: 21% of private credit deals were cov-lite in 2025, up from 4% in 2023, potentially reducing equity cure relevance even in private credit [VERIFY: time-sensitive — private credit cov-lite trend data from PitchBook] (Source: Proskauer Private Credit Deep Dives; PitchBook, February 2026).

### Cross-default versus cross-acceleration

The market is split. **Cross-default** triggers upon default (or entitlement to acceleration) under another debt agreement exceeding a threshold amount — even if the other lender has not actually accelerated. **Cross-acceleration** triggers only upon actual acceleration or payment default — a more borrower-friendly standard. Cross-acceleration prevails in leveraged loans with strong sponsors and in high-yield bonds; cross-default is more common in private credit and middle-market deals. The threshold amount is negotiated deal-by-deal and typically calibrated to the borrower's overall debt structure (Source: HSF Kramer; Jolly Contrarian; CABRI).

**Material adverse change** as a standalone EOD is "far from what would be considered market" in US leveraged finance [US] but remains more common in LMA-style European documentation [UK/EU]. Only one Delaware case (Akorn v. Fresenius, 2018) has ever found that a MAC occurred (51% decline in profits over five quarters). Despite COVID clearly meeting the MAC threshold for many borrowers, lenders almost universally chose not to invoke it due to reputational and systemic concerns (Source: Brownstein Hyatt; Ashurst; ABF Journal).

### Cross-border insolvency triggers [JURISDICTION-SPECIFIC]

Default provisions tied to insolvency events diverge across jurisdictions. US credit agreements typically reference Chapter 7/Chapter 11 filing thresholds and federal bankruptcy court proceedings. UK/EU documentation references administration, winding-up petitions, schemes of arrangement, and (post-2019) the EU Restructuring Directive's preventive restructuring frameworks. Cross-border deals frequently include parallel insolvency triggers for both US and European events, with the grace period for involuntary proceedings (typically 60–90 days) applying to both jurisdictions. Loan agents administering multi-jurisdictional facilities must track EOD definitions against the applicable insolvency regime for each obligor's jurisdiction of incorporation.

## 10. Credit agreement structure reflects decades of accumulating complexity

### The anatomy of a leveraged loan credit agreement [US]

Modern leveraged loan credit agreements for large-cap sponsor-backed deals run **150–300+ pages** for the credit agreement alone, with additional security documents, intercreditor agreements, and ancillaries. Harvard/NBER research (Ivashina & Vallée) found the average credit agreement includes **80 distinct carve-outs and 8 deductibles** to negative covenants. The LSTA's suite of documents now exceeds 200 items. Growth drivers include: adoption of HY bond-style covenant packages, proliferation of carve-outs and builder mechanisms, regulatory additions (SOFR transition, sanctions, anti-corruption, bail-in, ERISA), sponsor precedent accumulation, and protective responses to LME exploitation (Source: IOSCO FR03/2024; Ivashina & Vallée, NBER; LSTA).

The standard article-by-article structure follows a well-established pattern:

- **Article I (Definitions and Accounting Terms)** — Where practitioners note "the bodies are buried." Key defined terms driving interpretation include EBITDA and its add-backs, Permitted Indebtedness, Material Adverse Effect, Change of Control, Required Lenders, and Restricted/Unrestricted Subsidiaries.
- **Article II (Commitments and Borrowings)** — Facility amounts, borrowing procedures, rate selection (SOFR + margin; for rate mechanics see Doc 3, §§ 1–3), prepayment provisions (voluntary and mandatory excess cash flow sweeps), swingline and LC subfacilities, extension and incremental facility mechanics.
- **Article III (Representations and Warranties)** — Organization, authorization, financial statement accuracy, no material adverse change, compliance with laws/sanctions/anti-corruption, solvency, ERISA, environmental, tax, and IP matters. *For detailed treatment of ERISA considerations in syndicated lending — including the plan asset regulation, the 25% significant participation test, and applicable PTEs — see Doc 5, §5.*
- **Article IV (Conditions Precedent)** — Closing conditions (executed documents, certificates, opinions, KYC, fees) and subsequent borrowing conditions (no default, representation bring-down).
- **Article V (Affirmative Covenants)** — Financial reporting (annual audited within 60–90 days, quarterly unaudited within 45 days), compliance certificates, default notices, maintenance of existence, insurance, inspection rights.
- **Article VI (Negative Covenants)** — Restrictions on indebtedness, liens, investments, mergers, asset sales, restricted payments, affiliate transactions. Financial maintenance covenants (or in cov-lite deals, springing covenants tested only when revolving usage exceeds 25–40%).
- **Article VII (Events of Default)** — Non-payment, covenant breach, representation breach, cross-default, insolvency, judgments, change of control, ERISA events, invalidity of loan documents. *For detailed treatment of ERISA considerations in syndicated lending — including the plan asset regulation, the 25% significant participation test, and applicable PTEs — see Doc 5, §5.*
- **Article VIII (Agent Provisions)** — Appointment and authority, exculpatory language, reliance rights, resignation/replacement, lender acknowledgment of independent credit decisions.
- **Article IX (Miscellaneous)** — Amendments/waivers, assignments and participations, notices, governing law, jurisdiction, expense reimbursement, indemnification, confidentiality, erroneous payment provisions.

**Key schedules** include the commitment schedule, existing indebtedness, existing liens, existing investments, material contracts, real property, subsidiaries/guarantors, and (in some deals) the disqualified lenders list. **Key exhibits** include forms of: assignment and assumption agreement, compliance certificate, borrowing request, interest election request, prepayment notice, and (if applicable) promissory notes and security agreements (Source: Lucosky Brookman, 2023; LSTA MCAPs; White & Case, June 2024).

### LMA structural divergence [UK/EU]

LMA-form leveraged facility agreements follow a broadly comparable article structure but with key architectural differences. Security is held on trust by a security trustee (rather than directly by a collateral agent), necessitating parallel debt mechanics in many continental European jurisdictions. Transfer provisions offer novation (via transfer certificates) as the primary mechanism alongside assignment, reflecting the English law tradition. The LMA intercreditor agreement is a standalone document with distinct structural features, including turnover provisions and a separate release and enforcement framework. Agent liability standards and indemnification provisions also differ from LSTA conventions, reflecting English law principles of duty of care. For a detailed comparison, see § 8 above.

### The Sue Wright framework [UK/EU]

Sue Wright, operating through Lexitus (suewrightonline.com), is a prominent educator in loan documentation whose courses use the LMA loan agreement as the basis for structural analysis. Her framework for credit agreement analysis organizes the agreement into six analytical categories: (1) **the parties and the facility** — who borrows, who lends, what is the structure; (2) **the economics** — interest, fees, pricing, repayment; (3) **protection and monitoring** — representations, warranties, and covenants; (4) **default and enforcement** — events of default and remedies; (5) **transfer and administration** — assignment/novation, agency provisions; and (6) **boilerplate and miscellaneous** — governing law, jurisdiction, amendments, confidentiality [VERIFY: event-driven — the specific six categories require confirmation from her published works, as they are not fully available from public sources] (Source: suewrightonline.com/Lexitus course descriptions).

## 11. Intercreditor Agreements: Agent Operational Considerations [INTERNATIONAL]

Intercreditor agreements govern the relationship between different classes of creditors sharing the same borrower and often the same collateral. For the administrative agent, understanding intercreditor terms is essential because they modify payment waterfalls, restrict enforcement actions, and create obligations that override or supplement the credit agreement.

**Types of intercreditor agreements:**

*First-lien/second-lien intercreditor:*
Governs priority between first-lien (senior) and second-lien (junior) secured creditors. Key terms affecting agent operations:
- **Payment waterfall modification:** Second-lien lenders receive no principal payments until first-lien obligations are paid in full (the 'payment blockage' or 'standstill'). The agent distributing payments must apply the correct waterfall.
- **Enforcement standstill:** Second-lien lenders cannot take enforcement action (foreclose on collateral, accelerate) for a standstill period (typically 90-180 days) after a default, giving first-lien lenders time to act.
- **Release provisions:** If first-lien lenders release collateral in connection with an asset sale or other permitted transaction, second-lien liens are automatically released.
- **DIP financing consent:** Second-lien lenders may be required to consent to DIP financing that primes their liens in bankruptcy.

*Agreement Among Lenders (AAL) — Unitranche:*
Governs the internal allocation between first-out and last-out tranches in a unitranche facility (see Doc 1, §9). The borrower sees a single facility; the AAL governs how economics and control are split between tranches. Key features:
- **Blended rate allocation:** The AAL specifies what rate each tranche receives — first-out gets a lower rate, last-out gets a higher rate, blending to the borrower's all-in rate
- **Voting control:** Typically, the last-out tranche has voting control subject to customary sacred rights protections for the first-out
- **Enforcement:** The first-out tranche controls remedies upon default, with buy-out rights available to the last-out tranche
- **Agent's obligation:** The agent must understand the AAL to correctly calculate interest allocations, but the AAL itself is typically confidential — the borrower may not be party to it

*Senior/mezzanine intercreditor:*
Similar to first-lien/second-lien but between senior secured and unsecured mezzanine debt. Payment subordination is typically by contract rather than lien priority. Key difference: mezzanine intercreditors may include equity conversion features (equity kickers, warrants) that give mezzanine lenders upside participation.

**Agent operational responsibilities under intercreditor agreements:**
1. **Payment application:** Apply payments in the correct priority per the intercreditor waterfall — errors can create inter-creditor disputes and agent liability
2. **Notice forwarding:** Certain notices must be simultaneously delivered to all creditor groups; intercreditor agreements typically require the agent to forward default notices, acceleration notices, and enforcement notices to all parties
3. **Collateral release coordination:** When collateral is released, the agent must verify that the release complies with BOTH the credit agreement and the intercreditor agreement
4. **Enforcement coordination:** In default scenarios, the agent must understand which creditor group controls enforcement and the applicable standstill periods
5. **Amendment restrictions:** Some intercreditor agreements restrict amendments to the underlying credit agreements that would adversely affect the other creditor group — the agent should flag proposed amendments that may trigger intercreditor restrictions

[PRIVATE CREDIT] Intercreditor complexity is particularly acute in unitranche structures where the AAL governs internal economics. The agent must correctly allocate interest between first-out and last-out tranches while maintaining the appearance of a single facility to the borrower. AAL enforceability in bankruptcy remains untested — a key risk factor for agents administering unitranche facilities.

**Operational tip:** During deal onboarding, extract the key intercreditor terms and build them into the servicing platform's waterfall logic. Intercreditor provisions are easy to overlook in day-to-day operations but create significant liability exposure when they matter — which is precisely when the borrower is in distress and all parties are paying close attention.

## Conclusion: five themes shaping the next phase

The credit agreement landscape as of early 2026 is defined by five interconnected dynamics. First, **the Serta/Mitel divergence** — with the Fifth Circuit closing the "open market purchase" loophole while New York courts upheld plain "purchase" language — means the specific contractual text is more determinative than ever, and practitioners must draft for both jurisdictions simultaneously. Second, **LME blocker adoption is widespread but inconsistently effective**: Serta blockers appear in over 70% of loans, yet CreditSights and Octus repeatedly flag exceptions that undermine their protective value. Third, **EBITDA add-backs continue to inflate leverage** by roughly 30% above realized performance, with S&P data showing the gap is persistent and not fully priced into spreads. Fourth, **cooperation agreements have emerged as lenders' primary defensive tool**, with borrowers counter-attacking through anti-cooperation provisions and expanded DQ lists — setting up potential antitrust challenges like the Optimum Communications lawsuit. Fifth, **convergence between US and European documentation** continues to accelerate, with European large-cap deals increasingly adopting NY law-style covenant packages even within LMA-form loan agreements, blurring jurisdictional distinctions that once provided clear analytical shortcuts.

For a loan agent analyzing credit agreements, the practical implication is clear: every provision must be read in light of how it has been exploited or defended in recent litigation, and the interplay between baskets, exceptions, defined terms, and voting mechanics matters far more than any individual clause read in isolation.
