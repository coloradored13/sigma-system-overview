# Negotiation Coach

Communication coaching grounded in Chris Voss's tactical negotiation framework.
Not just "rewrite this nicer" — this skill analyzes the power dynamics, identifies
what the other side actually needs, and applies specific techniques to move the
conversation toward your goal.

## When This Skill Fires

| Situation | What You Get |
|---|---|
| Drafting a message where stakes matter (fee letter, pushback, objection) | Analysis of dynamics + rewrite using specific techniques |
| "How do I handle this?" with a difficult person/situation | Technique recommendation + example phrasing |
| Preparing for a negotiation (salary, vendor, contract terms) | Full prep sheet: accusation audit, calibrated questions, one-sheet |
| Reviewing a message you received — "how do I respond to this?" | Analysis of their tactics + response options |
| De-escalating tension (angry stakeholder, heated Slack thread, team conflict) | Labeling + tactical empathy approach |

## Core Techniques

### 1. Tactical Empathy
Understanding their feelings and perspective — not to agree, but to disarm.
This is the foundation. Every other technique builds on it.

**How to apply:**
- Before writing anything, answer: What is the other side feeling? What pressures are they under? What do they actually need (vs what they're asking for)?
- Demonstrate understanding BEFORE making your ask
- "I understand this creates additional work for your team" → then your request

**Not sympathy.** You're not agreeing or being soft. You're showing you see their world, which makes them feel heard, which makes them listen.

### 2. Labeling
Name their emotion or concern out loud. This defuses it.

**Format:** Always start with third-person phrasing:
- "It seems like..."
- "It sounds like..."
- "It looks like..."

**Never** "I think you feel..." — that makes it about you.

**Examples:**
- "It seems like there's concern about timeline pressure on your side."
- "It sounds like the pricing structure is the main sticking point."
- "It looks like this has been frustrating for your team."

**After labeling: pause.** Let them respond. The label does the work — don't rush past it.

### 3. Mirroring
Repeat the last 1-3 critical words they said, with upward inflection.

**What it does:** Signals you're listening. Encourages them to elaborate. Buys you time.

**Example:**
- Them: "We just can't make that work with our current budget constraints."
- You: "Budget constraints?"
- Them: [elaborates with the real issue]

**Then: silence for 4+ seconds.** Let the mirror work.

### 4. Calibrated Questions
Open-ended questions starting with "How" or "What" that give the other side the illusion of control while steering toward your goal.

**Power questions:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (gentle pushback without saying no)
- "What about this is important to you?"
- "How can we make this work for both sides?"
- "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with this?"
- "How does this affect the rest of your team?"
- "What happens if we don't reach agreement?"

**Never "Why" — it sounds accusatory.**

**The goal:** Make them solve YOUR problem. When they answer "how can we make this work," they're now invested in finding a solution that works for you.

### 5. Accusation Audit
Before the other side can voice their objections, list every negative thing they might think about you or your position. Say it first.

**Why it works:** Naming the worst defuses it. If you say it, they can't use it against you. It feels disarming and honest.

**Format:**
"You're probably thinking [worst thing]. You might feel like [second worst thing]. It may seem like [third worst thing]."

Then pause. Let them respond. They'll almost always soften: "No, it's not that bad..."

**Example — fee letter negotiation:**
"You're probably thinking our fees are higher than what you've seen from other providers. You might feel like we're not being flexible on structure. It may seem like we're prioritizing our revenue over the relationship."

Then: present your actual position, which now sounds reasonable by comparison.

### 6. Getting to "That's Right"
"That's right" = breakthrough. They feel fully understood.
"You're right" = dismissal. They're trying to end the conversation.

**How to get "that's right":**
1. Use active listening (summarize what they've said)
2. Label their emotions accurately
3. Paraphrase their position back to them without judgment
4. When they say "that's right" — they're now open to your perspective

### 7. Ackerman Method (Price/Terms Negotiation)
For any negotiation with numbers:

1. Set your target (what you actually want)
2. Open at 65% of target
3. Three calibrated raises: 85%, 95%, 100%
4. Use empathy and "no" between each raise
5. Final number: use precise, non-round amounts ($37,893 not $38,000)
6. At your final number, throw in a non-monetary item to signal you're at your limit

### 8. Negotiation Styles
Identify their style to calibrate your approach:

| Style | How They Operate | How to Handle |
|---|---|---|
| **Analyst** | Methodical, hates surprises, needs data and time. Silence = thinking. | Give them data. Don't rush. Respect their process. |
| **Accommodator** | Relationship-focused, wants to be liked, fears conflict. Silence = anger. | Build rapport first. Don't mistake friendliness for agreement. |
| **Assertive** | Wants to be heard, values respect, direct. Silence = you have nothing to say. | Mirror and listen first. They'll only hear you after they feel heard. |

## Modes

### Draft Mode — "Help me write this"
User has a message to send and wants it to be tactically effective.

1. **Analyze the situation:** Who's the audience? What do they care about? What's the power dynamic? What's your goal?
2. **Identify applicable techniques:** Which of the 8 techniques above fit this situation?
3. **Draft with technique annotations:** Show the rewrite AND explain what each technique does and why you used it. The user should learn the technique, not just get a better draft.
4. **Offer alternatives:** If multiple approaches could work, show 2 — one that prioritizes the relationship, one that prioritizes the outcome. Name the tradeoff.

### Response Mode — "They said X, how do I respond?"
User received something and needs to navigate it.

1. **Decode their tactics:** What are they doing? (Anchoring? Deadline pressure? Emotional manipulation? Legitimate concern?)
2. **Identify their style:** Analyst, Accommodator, or Assertive?
3. **Recommend response technique:** Mirror? Label? Calibrated question? Accusation audit?
4. **Draft the response** with annotations explaining the strategic purpose of each element.

### Prep Mode — "I have a negotiation coming up"
Full preparation for an upcoming negotiation.

Build a **one-sheet** (Voss's preparation format):

```
## Negotiation One-Sheet

### Goal
Target outcome: [specific, ambitious but legitimate]
Best case: [what does a win look like]
Worst acceptable: [walk-away point]

### Accusation Audit
1. "You're probably thinking..."
2. "You might feel like..."
3. "It may seem like..."

### Calibrated Questions (prepare 3-5)
1. "How can we..."
2. "What would it take to..."
3. "How does this affect..."

### Labels (prepare 3)
1. "It seems like..."
2. "It sounds like..."
3. "It looks like..."

### Their Style
Analyst / Accommodator / Assertive — and what that means for approach

### Black Swans (unknowns to uncover)
1. What don't I know about their pressures/constraints?
2. What hidden motivation might change everything?
3. What would make them say yes immediately?

### Ackerman Plan (if price/terms)
Target: $X
Open: $Y (65%)
Raises: $Z1 (85%), $Z2 (95%), $Z3 (100%)
Non-monetary kicker: [what to offer at final number]
```

### De-escalation Mode — "This is getting heated"
For tense situations that need to be calmed before they can be resolved.

1. **Label the emotion immediately.** "It seems like this has been really frustrating."
2. **Don't defend.** Defending escalates. Labeling de-escalates.
3. **Use the late-night FM DJ voice.** Slow, calm, downward inflection. In writing: shorter sentences, lower intensity, no exclamation marks.
4. **Mirror their core concern.** Show you heard the substance, not just the emotion.
5. **Ask one calibrated question.** "What would a good outcome look like for you?" — redirects from venting to problem-solving.

## Gotchas

- **Techniques without genuine empathy are manipulation.** The framework only works if you actually try to understand the other side. People detect fake empathy instantly.
- **Don't stack techniques.** One mirror + one label per exchange is plenty. Deploying every technique in one message sounds like a hostage negotiation, not a professional conversation.
- **"How am I supposed to do that?" is nuclear.** Use it only when you genuinely need to push back on an unreasonable demand. In routine conversations it comes across as passive-aggressive.
- **Silence is the hardest technique.** After a mirror or label, WAIT. Most people fill the silence with the information you needed.
- **Not everything is a negotiation.** If a colleague asks you to grab coffee, don't label their emotions. Save the toolkit for situations where stakes and dynamics actually warrant it.
- **The Ackerman method is for price negotiation, not relationship building.** Don't apply it to salary conversations with your own team or situations where trust matters more than the number.
